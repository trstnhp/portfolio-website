Gro

a plant based webstore

The Idea

Make plant buying simple. Give the user the option to tell us if they have lots of light, or want a plant that isn’t so fussy... or any other parameter, to make sure they get a plant they will love, and they can keep alive.

The Process

Building a Brand

Lots of decisions were made early on around how I wanted users to experience Gro as a brand. From the way we speak, to the images we choose and the colours and fonts we use. All of this helps define the brand as well as gives me a playbook to design by later on.

writing style

We always refer to ourselves as a we. Don’t we? We keep things light, clear and friendly. Informal, but not too casual Light - plants need light to grow. So let’s keep our tone that way. Simple words, easy to understand. Clear - plants can be confusing, so we can’t be. Keep things as simple as they can be, and PLEASE don’t use jargon. Friendly - welcome people to our site, and invite them to return when they leave. And all along their journey through our site keep the tone warm.

image style

the two main image types we use are product images and lifestyle image. with product images we keep a light (preferably greyscale) background. With lifestyle images we use the room type that the user has searched by (bathroom, bedroom etc.), it needs to be well lit (unless user has searched by low lit plants), the room need to be well styled, but look lived in and not too sterile.

I already had a brief to work from - so I knew what our users needed. But these User Flows helped figure out how we would get them there.

Wireframing

Low Fidelity Wireframes

A lot of the changes that were made between low and medium fidelity screens were based on user feedback about having more space to see the plants (on 3rd screen) and utilising the “add to basket” banner (on 4th screen) in the 5th screen as a “checkout” banner to keep consistency.

Medium Fidelity Wireframes

The final round of user feedback was gathered after the med fidelity screens were designed. Users suggested breaking up the home screen (2nd screen) with larger images along the top to highlight any new article or sale to new or returning customers.
